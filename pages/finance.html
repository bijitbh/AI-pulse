NatWest taps Cleareye.ai for trade finance ops

FinExtra Fri, 29 May 2026 00:01:00 GMT

NatWest has entered into a partnership with Cleareye.ai, a specialist in AI-powered trade finance technology, to revamp its trade operations and strengthen financial crime controls. Trade finance has long been burdened by manual, paper-intensive processes, making it a prime target for AI-driven automation. Cleareye.ai's platform uses machine learning to ingest and analyse trade documents such as letters of credit, bills of lading, and invoices, significantly reducing processing times and human error. By automating document checking and compliance screening, NatWest aims to improve operational efficiency across its corporate and institutional banking divisions. The partnership also addresses the increasingly complex regulatory landscape around financial crime in trade corridors, including sanctions screening and AML compliance. NatWest joins a growing cohort of major banks deploying AI to modernise legacy trade finance infrastructure. The integration is expected to reduce turnaround times for trade transactions while freeing compliance teams to focus on higher-risk cases. Cleareye.ai has been gaining traction with tier-one banks seeking to reduce the cost and risk associated with manual trade document processing. For NatWest's corporate clients, faster and more accurate trade processing can translate directly into working capital improvements. The deal underscores how AI is becoming a competitive differentiator in wholesale transaction banking, particularly as global trade volumes recover and complexity increases. This move is also consistent with broader industry trends pushing banks to modernise trade finance as part of digital transformation agendas.

Impact Takeaways

  • AI-powered document processing in trade finance can dramatically cut turnaround times and reduce compliance risk for corporate banking clients.
  • NatWest's deployment signals that tier-one banks are accelerating AI adoption specifically in trade finance operations and financial crime controls.
  • Practitioners should monitor how AI-driven compliance screening is reshaping AML and sanctions workflows in trade corridors.
  • The partnership highlights the growing market for specialist fintech vendors targeting high-volume, document-intensive wholesale banking processes.

The platform targets operational workflows including loan processing, onboarding, compliance checks, and back-office automation, areas where banks still rely heavily on manual effort. The Series A raise signals robust venture capital confidence in the application of agentic AI to regulated financial services environments. Banks and credit unions face mounting pressure to reduce operational costs while maintaining compliance, making automated workflow agents an attractive proposition. Saris differentiates itself by building governance and auditability into its agentic architecture, addressing a key concern for regulated institutions deploying autonomous AI. The funding will likely be used to expand enterprise sales, deepen integrations with core banking systems, and scale the platform's AI capabilities. The raise comes as major banks are actively piloting agentic AI internally, increasing the competitive urgency for smaller institutions to access similar capabilities through vendors. Agentic platforms like Saris could also play a role in accelerating straight-through processing in payments and trade finance. The investment also underscores the broader shift in financial services AI from assistive tools to autonomous execution layers. For wholesale banking practitioners, the rise of agentic platforms raises important questions about workflow redesign, liability, and human oversight frameworks.

Impact Takeaways

  • Agentic AI platforms are attracting significant venture investment, signalling a shift from AI-assist to AI-execute in banking operations.
  • Banks and credit unions evaluating AI vendors should scrutinise governance and auditability features as autonomous agents take on compliance-sensitive workflows.
  • The funding environment for fintech AI infrastructure remains strong, suggesting continued rapid product development in this space.
  • Wholesale banking operations teams should begin assessing where agentic automation could replace or augment current manual processes.

Why Corporate Treasuries Are Increasing AI Investments

Global Finance Tue, 26 May 2026 15:23:34 +0000

A new Bain & Company survey of senior financial executives reveals a nuanced picture of AI adoption within corporate treasury organisations. Only 48% of respondents reported improvements in speed and cycle time from AI investments, while just 34% have seen headcount efficiencies and cost reductions. Despite these modest early returns, the vast majority of enterprises have continued or accelerated discussions around AI investment in treasury over the past 12 months. The survey suggests that many treasury functions are still in the experimentation or early deployment phase, with full-scale integration yet to materialise. Key use cases being explored include cash flow forecasting, liquidity management, FX hedging automation, and payment reconciliation. Bain's findings highlight a persistent gap between AI ambition and realised operational value, which practitioners describe as an execution and change management challenge rather than a technology limitation. Banks providing treasury management services to corporates face both opportunity and pressure as clients demand AI-enhanced capabilities from their banking partners. The report notes that enterprises with more mature data infrastructure tend to see better AI outcomes, pointing to the importance of data quality investments as a precursor to AI returns. CFOs and treasurers are increasingly holding vendors and internal teams accountable for demonstrable ROI rather than adopting AI for its own sake. The survey also flags that regulatory scrutiny of AI-driven treasury decisions is increasing, particularly in areas like liquidity stress testing. For wholesale banking professionals, the findings underscore both the growing client demand for AI-powered treasury services and the importance of helping corporates bridge the implementation gap.

Impact Takeaways

  • Less than half of treasury AI investments are delivering measurable speed or cost improvements, highlighting a significant execution gap that banks and vendors must help clients close.
  • Data infrastructure quality is emerging as the primary determinant of AI success in treasury, making it a priority investment area.
  • Banks offering treasury management services should expect corporate clients to demand AI-enhanced capabilities with demonstrable ROI.
  • Regulatory scrutiny of AI in treasury decisions is growing, particularly around liquidity management and stress testing.

The article highlights how innovative payment rails are enabling faster cross-border settlement, which is critical for facilitating intra-African trade under frameworks such as the African Continental Free Trade Area (AfCFTA). Interoperability between payment systems remains a central challenge, and AI is being used to bridge legacy infrastructure with modern digital rails. Regulatory developments across African markets are shaping how banks deploy AI, with a growing emphasis on compliance automation and regtech integration. Standard Bank's approach underscores the role of large regional banks in acting as infrastructure providers for both corporate and institutional clients engaged in cross-border transactions. The piece also touches on how AI-enhanced know-your-customer and AML processes are reducing friction for trade finance clients while maintaining compliance standards. For global wholesale banks with African operations, the insights are directly relevant to transaction banking strategy, correspondent banking relationships, and trade finance corridor development. The conversation reflects a broader global trend of emerging market banks leapfrogging legacy infrastructure through AI-enabled payment modernisation. Practitioners in global transaction banking should note Africa's accelerating pace of payments innovation as both a competitive opportunity and a model for other developing market corridors.

Impact Takeaways

  • AI-driven fraud detection and transaction monitoring are becoming table stakes for banks operating across Africa's complex multi-corridor payments environment.
  • Interoperability enabled by AI is critical to unlocking the commercial potential of intra-African trade under AfCFTA for transaction banking teams.
  • Global banks with African correspondent or CIB operations should assess how AI-powered payment rails are reshaping competitive dynamics in the region.
  • Compliance automation and regtech integration are increasingly central to scalable transaction banking in fragmented regulatory environments.

Fiserv brings in Cognition's AI agent software engineer

FinExtra Fri, 29 May 2026 00:01:00 GMT

Fiserv, one of the world's largest financial technology providers serving banks and payment processors, has integrated Devin, an AI software engineering agent from vendor Cognition, into its development operations. Devin is designed to autonomously write, test, and debug code, enabling Fiserv to ship new product capabilities to its banking clients at a faster pace. This deployment represents one of the more concrete examples of an AI agent being embedded directly into the engineering workflow of a major financial services infrastructure provider. Fiserv serves thousands of financial institutions globally, including many banks relying on its core banking, payments, and risk platforms, meaning accelerated product delivery has broad downstream implications. The use of an AI software engineer reflects a wider trend of financial technology firms augmenting or partially replacing human developers with autonomous coding agents. Cognition's Devin has been positioned as a full software engineering agent rather than a code completion tool, capable of handling end-to-end development tasks with minimal human intervention. For Fiserv's bank clients, faster feature delivery could translate into quicker access to AI-powered banking capabilities, improved payment processing infrastructure, and accelerated compliance tooling updates. The deployment also raises questions about quality assurance, security review processes, and the governance of AI-generated code in regulated financial environments. Industry observers will be watching closely to assess whether AI-driven engineering at scale translates into measurable improvements in platform reliability and innovation speed. The move signals that AI agents are moving beyond front-office and analytics applications into the core technology operations of financial services firms. For wholesale banking technology leaders, this development is a leading indicator of how AI will reshape software procurement, vendor capability assessment, and internal engineering strategies.

Impact Takeaways

  • AI software engineering agents are entering production at major fintech vendors, with direct implications for how quickly new banking capabilities reach institutional clients.
  • Banks relying on third-party platforms like Fiserv should engage vendors on how AI-generated code is governed, tested, and secured before deployment.
  • The use of autonomous coding agents in regulated financial infrastructure raises important questions about liability, auditability, and change management.
  • Technology leaders in wholesale banking should factor AI-driven engineering velocity into their vendor selection and roadmap planning processes.
