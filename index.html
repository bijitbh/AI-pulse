01

How AI Is Rewriting Credit Decisioning in Real Time

PYMNTS AI

AI is fundamentally disrupting the decades-old model of static scorecards and rigid rule-based credit decisioning. As transactions migrate to real-time digital channels, issuers must now evaluate risk, intent, and context instantaneously. This shift represents one of the most significant structural changes in credit underwriting in a generation.

02

In Payments, the AI Race Is Also a Governance Test

PYMNTS AI

AI is rapidly embedding itself across payments infrastructure — from fraud detection to compliance and customer experience — but governance frameworks are struggling to keep pace. The question of who is accountable for AI-driven decisions is now the defining challenge for financial institutions. Regulators and executives alike are grappling with how to manage risk in systems that operate faster than human oversight.

03

Acquirers Say Risk and Readiness Are Slowing Agentic Commerce

PYMNTS AI

A new study finds that most payment acquirers believe their core infrastructure is ready for AI-agent-driven commerce, but merchant readiness and unresolved risk frameworks are creating significant bottlenecks. Agentic commerce — where AI systems autonomously initiate and complete transactions — is advancing faster than the ecosystem is prepared to handle. The gap between acquirer confidence and merchant capability is emerging as a critical friction point.

04

The IPO Window Is Open—A Crack

Global Finance

The IPO market is showing signs of renewed activity but remains constrained by macroeconomic volatility and shifting investor sentiment around AI valuations. Deal flow is trickling rather than surging as underwriters and issuers navigate a risk-off environment. AI sector uncertainty is playing a notable role in reshaping how capital markets participants evaluate growth-stage listings.

05

US Regulators Lift Veil On Digital Assets

Global Finance

The SEC and CFTC have issued new guidance clarifying how digital assets are classified under existing regulatory frameworks, providing greater — if still incomplete — certainty for financial institutions. While the guidance stops short of comprehensive legislation, it gives banks and capital markets participants clearer parameters for digital asset activities. Major financial institutions are now better positioned to expand institutional digital asset services within defined regulatory boundaries.
