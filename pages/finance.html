How AI Is Rewriting Credit Decisioning in Real Time

PYMNTS AI Mon, 13 Apr 2026 08:01:11 +0000

For decades, the credit industry has depended on static scorecards and rigid if-then rule systems designed for a slower financial environment. That legacy model is now under significant strain as digital transactions proliferate and consumer expectations for instant decisions rise. AI is enabling issuers to move beyond historical credit data and fixed rules to evaluate risk dynamically and contextually. Machine learning models can now ingest vast streams of behavioral, transactional, and alternative data to produce real-time credit decisions. This shift allows lenders to assess intent and contextual signals that traditional models could never capture. The transformation is not merely technical but structural, altering underwriting workflows, compliance frameworks, and risk management strategies. Real-time decisioning reduces both false positives and false negatives in credit approvals, potentially expanding financial access for underserved populations. Institutions adopting AI-powered credit engines must also contend with model explainability requirements under fair lending regulations. The article frames this as an ongoing series, signaling that AI-driven credit transformation will continue to evolve across multiple dimensions. Risk professionals must now balance speed and accuracy against transparency and regulatory accountability. For financial institutions, the competitive pressure to modernize credit decisioning with AI is rapidly becoming unavoidable.

Impact Takeaways

  • Static credit scoring models are being replaced by AI systems capable of evaluating risk, intent, and context in milliseconds, fundamentally changing underwriting.
  • AI-powered credit decisioning can reduce approval errors and expand access to credit for previously underserved consumer segments.
  • Financial institutions must address model explainability and fair lending compliance as AI moves deeper into credit workflows.
  • The shift demands new infrastructure investments and risk management frameworks to support real-time, data-rich decision engines.

In Payments, the AI Race Is Also a Governance Test

PYMNTS AI Tue, 07 Apr 2026 08:00:34 +0000

Artificial intelligence is accelerating into every layer of the payments ecosystem, including fraud detection, transaction approvals, personalized offers, compliance support, and real-time risk management. The pace of adoption is described as unusual, reflecting the competitive urgency firms feel to deploy AI capabilities before rivals. However, the article identifies a critical and underexamined challenge: governance. As AI systems make increasingly consequential decisions in payments, questions about accountability, oversight, and control remain largely unsettled across the industry. Regulatory bodies are beginning to scrutinize how financial institutions manage and audit AI-driven processes, particularly those affecting consumers. The governance gap — the distance between the speed of AI deployment and the maturity of oversight frameworks — represents a systemic risk for payment companies. Firms that race to deploy AI without robust governance structures may face regulatory backlash, operational failures, or reputational damage. The article highlights that internal governance structures, including who owns AI risk and how decisions are reviewed, vary widely across organizations. Industry professionals are being urged to treat governance not as a compliance checkbox but as a strategic imperative. The intersection of AI capability and accountability will likely define competitive positioning in the payments sector over the next several years. Firms that get governance right may gain a durable advantage in regulatory trust and operational resilience.

Impact Takeaways

  • AI adoption in payments is outpacing governance readiness, creating systemic risk for firms that deploy without adequate oversight structures.
  • Regulatory scrutiny of AI-driven payment decisions is intensifying, making accountability frameworks a competitive and compliance necessity.
  • Organizations must clearly define internal ownership of AI risk to avoid operational and reputational exposure.
  • Governance maturity — not just AI capability — will increasingly differentiate leaders from laggards in the payments industry.

Acquirers Say Risk and Readiness Are Slowing Agentic Commerce

PYMNTS AI Tue, 24 Mar 2026 08:00:35 +0000

A new industry study titled 'How Acquirers Prepare for Agentic Commerce' examines how payment acquirers are positioning themselves as AI agents take on larger roles in shopping and purchasing decisions. The report finds that a substantial majority of acquirers — approximately 80% — believe the core payments infrastructure is already capable of supporting agent-led transactions. However, the study also reveals a meaningful gap: merchant readiness lags considerably behind acquirer confidence. This divergence threatens to slow the broader adoption of agentic commerce despite infrastructure being largely in place. Risk concerns are identified as a primary factor holding back progress, particularly around how to authenticate, authorize, and manage liability for AI-initiated transactions. Traditional fraud and chargeback frameworks were designed for human-initiated purchases and may not map cleanly onto autonomous agent behavior. Acquirers are beginning to develop new protocols, but standardization across the ecosystem remains elusive. The article underscores that the agentic commerce transition is not purely a technology problem but an industry coordination challenge. Merchants who are slow to adapt risk being bypassed as AI agents increasingly favor frictionless, agent-optimized checkout experiences. For payment professionals, the findings suggest that the window to shape agentic commerce standards is open now but may close quickly. Proactive engagement with acquirers and technology partners will be critical for merchants seeking to participate in the next phase of digital commerce.

Impact Takeaways

  • Most acquirers report infrastructure readiness for agentic commerce, but merchant preparedness remains a critical gap slowing industry-wide adoption.
  • Existing fraud, authentication, and chargeback frameworks are not designed for AI-initiated transactions and require significant adaptation.
  • Payment professionals must engage now with acquirers and standard-setting bodies to shape agentic commerce protocols before they solidify.
  • Merchants that delay readiness risk exclusion from AI-agent-driven purchasing flows as the agentic commerce ecosystem matures.

How AI Killed Information Asymmetry in B2B Procurement

PYMNTS AI Mon, 30 Mar 2026 15:33:26 +0000

Artificial intelligence is transforming B2B sales dynamics by eliminating the information asymmetry that traditionally favored sellers over buyers in procurement negotiations. For decades, B2B sales teams held advantages in pricing knowledge, competitive benchmarking, and product positioning that procurement buyers could not easily close. AI tools are now putting comparable intelligence directly in the hands of procurement leaders, fundamentally altering the negotiating landscape. The article argues that what is disappearing is not friction but the traditional B2B sales process itself, as AI enables buyers to evaluate proposals, assess vendor alternatives, and model contract terms with unprecedented speed and depth. For financial services firms — including banks, insurers, and fintech providers — that sell to enterprise clients, this shift demands a rethinking of go-to-market strategies and value propositions. Sales teams that relied on information advantages to justify pricing or differentiate offerings will find those levers increasingly ineffective. The new competitive battleground is value delivery and trust, not information control. AI-powered procurement tools are also accelerating deal cycles in some cases and extending scrutiny in others, creating unpredictable dynamics for financial services sales organizations. Firms must invest in sales enablement tools that match the analytical sophistication now available to their buyers. Pricing transparency and demonstrable ROI are becoming baseline expectations rather than differentiators. Financial services providers that adapt their commercial models to this new buyer intelligence environment will be better positioned to win and retain enterprise relationships.

Impact Takeaways

  • AI has eliminated the information advantages B2B sellers once held, forcing financial services firms to rethink pricing strategies and value propositions.
  • Procurement teams now use AI to benchmark proposals and assess vendors with sophistication that rivals or exceeds traditional sales intelligence.
  • Financial services sales organizations must shift from information-based selling to value-based and trust-based commercial models.
  • Faster, more analytically rigorous procurement cycles demand that financial services providers invest in AI-enabled sales tools to remain competitive.
