#1

'What a joke': Github Copilot's new token-based billing spurs consternation among devs

TechCrunch AI Sat, 30 May 2026 16:30:00 +0000

GitHub Copilot, once celebrated as a game-changer for software development, is now facing significant pushback from its core user base over a new token-based billing model. Previously offered at a flat monthly rate, the tool is transitioning to a consumption-based pricing structure that charges developers based on the number of tokens processed. Many developers argue this change fundamentally undermines the appeal of the product, as heavy users — exactly the professionals who rely on it most — will see their costs skyrocket. Reactions across developer forums and social media have been swift and largely negative, with terms like 'betrayal' and 'bait and switch' appearing frequently. The shift mirrors broader industry moves by AI providers to recoup the enormous compute costs associated with running large language models at scale. Microsoft, which owns GitHub, has not publicly detailed the exact pricing tiers but the announcement has already triggered comparisons to competitors like Cursor and Windsurf. Some developers are actively evaluating open-source alternatives, including locally hosted models, to avoid escalating subscription fees. The backlash also reignites the broader conversation about the sustainability of AI tool pricing and whether the 'free or cheap' introductory era is definitively over. Industry analysts note that this could accelerate fragmentation in the AI coding assistant market as developers diversify their toolchains. For enterprises that have standardized on Copilot, budget planning for AI tooling will need to be revisited urgently. The episode underscores the growing tension between AI providers' cost structures and developers' expectations set during the product's early growth phase.

Impact Takeaways

  • Developers heavily reliant on GitHub Copilot should model their expected token consumption immediately to forecast cost increases under the new billing structure.
  • The shift may accelerate adoption of open-source or self-hosted coding AI alternatives, particularly among cost-conscious individual developers and small teams.
  • Enterprise procurement and engineering teams need to revisit AI tooling budgets and evaluate competitor products like Cursor, Windsurf, or locally hosted models.
  • This signals a broader industry trend: the introductory flat-rate era for AI coding assistants is ending as providers move toward usage-based monetization.
#2

Coders are refusing to work without AI — and that could come back to bite them

TechCrunch AI Fri, 29 May 2026 22:14:22 +0000

A new wave of developer behavior is emerging where software engineers increasingly refuse to engage in coding tasks without AI assistance, treating tools like Copilot and Claude Code as non-negotiable requirements rather than optional aids. While this reflects the productivity gains these tools deliver, researchers are sounding alarms about a deepening dependency that could have serious long-term consequences. Studies cited in the report suggest that AI-assisted code, despite being produced faster, is not necessarily better code — it often contains subtle bugs, security vulnerabilities, and architectural shortcuts that human reviewers may miss. The concern is compounded by the fact that developers who rely heavily on AI may be losing the foundational reasoning skills needed to identify and fix such problems. Over time, this creates a feedback loop: AI-generated code is reviewed by developers who are less capable of catching AI-generated errors. Senior engineers and tech leads are reportedly beginning to flag this in hiring pipelines, noting that junior developers entering the workforce have dramatically different baseline skill sets than prior cohorts. The article draws a parallel to GPS dependency in navigation — useful until the system fails, at which point users lack the skills to orient themselves. For organizations, this raises urgent questions about code review processes, testing standards, and onboarding practices. Academic researchers are calling for more rigorous studies on the long-term cognitive effects of AI-assisted programming. The debate is also feeding into broader discussions about AI's impact on knowledge worker skills across industries. The story is generating significant professional discussion and has resonated deeply within engineering communities online.

Impact Takeaways

  • Engineering managers should audit code review processes to ensure AI-generated code is being critically evaluated, not rubber-stamped.
  • Organizations need to invest in deliberate skill-building programs for junior developers to ensure foundational coding competencies are maintained.
  • The growing dependency on AI coding tools creates new categories of technical debt and security risk that QA and DevSecOps teams must account for.
  • Hiring criteria and technical interviews may need to be restructured to assess a candidate's ability to reason about code independently of AI assistance.
#3

SoftBank says it will invest up to €75 billion to build French data centers

TechCrunch AI Sat, 30 May 2026 21:45:00 +0000

SoftBank has announced an ambitious plan to invest up to €75 billion in France to build and operate new data centers, targeting the development of up to 5 gigawatts of additional capacity. The announcement represents one of the most significant AI infrastructure investments ever made on European soil and positions France as a potential hub for AI compute in the region. The move is widely seen as a strategic bet on Europe's growing regulatory and political push for AI sovereignty, with several EU nations seeking to reduce dependence on US-based cloud and AI infrastructure providers. SoftBank's involvement also signals the Japanese conglomerate's continued aggressive pivot toward AI infrastructure following its earlier commitments in the United States through the Stargate initiative. The scale of the investment — equivalent to roughly $82 billion at current exchange rates — dwarfs many prior European data center announcements and will require substantial coordination with French energy authorities given the power demands involved. France's existing nuclear energy infrastructure makes it an attractive host for energy-intensive AI workloads, a factor likely central to SoftBank's site selection. The announcement comes amid a broader global race to secure AI compute capacity, with hyperscalers and sovereign wealth funds competing aggressively for land, power, and cooling resources. European regulators and policymakers are likely to scrutinize the deal given data sovereignty implications, though the investment is expected to be welcomed for its job creation and economic impact. Industry observers note that announcements of this magnitude do not always fully materialize, and execution timelines remain unclear. Nevertheless, the commitment further accelerates the transformation of AI infrastructure investment from a US-centric story to a genuinely global one.

Impact Takeaways

  • Enterprise technology buyers in Europe should monitor this investment closely, as it could meaningfully expand locally available AI compute and cloud options within the next several years.
  • The announcement accelerates the trend of AI infrastructure decentralization, with significant implications for data sovereignty and compliance planning for European organizations.
  • Energy availability and nuclear power infrastructure are emerging as decisive factors in where large-scale AI data center investments land globally.
  • For AI startups and cloud-native companies, a more competitive European infrastructure market could translate into lower compute costs and improved latency for EU-based deployments.
#4

What happens when companies become too AI-pilled?

TechCrunch AI Fri, 29 May 2026 17:57:05 +0000

The concept of 'AI psychosis' — coined by Box founder Aaron Levie — is gaining traction as a framework for understanding a troubling pattern in corporate decision-making: leaders who are deeply enthusiastic about AI making dramatic workforce and operational changes without adequately understanding the complexity of the roles they are eliminating. ClickUp's recent decision to cut 22% of its workforce and replace those functions with AI agents has become a high-profile case study in this phenomenon, drawing both admiration and sharp criticism. Tech sector layoffs in 2026 are already approaching the total seen throughout all of 2025, with AI-driven automation cited as a contributing factor in many announcements. Critics argue that the executives making these calls are often the least equipped to evaluate what their employees actually do on a day-to-day basis, leading to decisions that may look efficient on paper but create hidden operational risks. The video segment explores the broader cultural and business implications of organizations becoming so ideologically committed to AI transformation that they lose the ability to critically assess its limits. Panelists note that the gap between AI's theoretical capabilities and its real-world reliability in complex enterprise workflows remains significant. There are also growing concerns about the chilling effect on organizational knowledge and institutional memory when experienced workers are rapidly replaced. The discussion touches on historical parallels with past waves of automation-driven restructuring and whether the outcomes this time will be different. Investor pressure to demonstrate AI-driven efficiency gains is identified as a structural driver pushing companies toward potentially reckless adoption speeds. The episode reflects a maturing public discourse around AI in the workplace, moving beyond simple enthusiasm or fear toward more nuanced risk assessment.

Impact Takeaways

  • Business leaders should implement structured evaluation frameworks before replacing human roles with AI agents, including honest assessments of what institutional knowledge may be lost.
  • HR and organizational design teams need to be central stakeholders in AI transformation planning, not afterthoughts following executive decisions.
  • Companies that move too aggressively on AI-driven headcount reduction risk operational failures that may not surface until months after the restructuring.
  • Investor relations and communications teams should prepare for increased scrutiny of AI-driven layoff announcements as public and regulatory skepticism grows.
#5

Meta is reportedly developing an AI pendant

TechCrunch AI Sat, 30 May 2026 15:59:58 +0000

Meta is reportedly developing an AI-powered pendant device, marking a significant expansion of the company's wearable hardware strategy beyond its existing Ray-Ban smart glasses lineup. The pendant is said to function as an always-on AI companion, capable of ambient listening and real-time interaction, positioning it as a competitor to similar concepts explored by startups like Rewind and the now-defunct Humane AI Pin. The move reflects Meta's broader conviction that the next major computing platform will be AI-native wearables that integrate seamlessly into users' physical environments rather than requiring screen interaction. CEO Mark Zuckerberg has repeatedly signaled that hardware combined with AI represents one of the company's most important long-term bets. The pendant form factor is notable because it suggests Meta is exploring multiple hardware categories simultaneously — glasses for visual AI, earbuds for audio, and now a pendant for ambient and social contexts. Consumer interest in AI hardware has been mixed; the Humane AI Pin's high-profile failure demonstrated that execution, battery life, and genuine utility are critical hurdles. Meta's scale and existing AI infrastructure through its Llama model family give it structural advantages that pure-play AI hardware startups lack. Privacy advocates are expected to raise concerns given the always-on nature of such a device and Meta's contentious history with user data. The announcement will also intensify competition with Apple, Google, and Amazon, all of whom are developing or refining their own AI wearable strategies. The report has generated significant discussion in both consumer tech and AI research communities about the future of human-AI interaction paradigms.

Impact Takeaways

  • Product and UX teams developing AI applications should closely track Meta's wearable hardware roadmap, as new ambient form factors will require rethinking interaction design conventions.
  • Privacy officers and compliance teams at enterprises should begin scenario planning for workplaces where employees use always-on AI wearables that capture ambient conversations.
  • The AI hardware category is re-emerging as a serious investment and development area despite earlier high-profile failures — practitioners should evaluate integration opportunities early.
  • Meta's entry intensifies pressure on competitors including Apple and Google to accelerate their own AI wearable roadmaps, likely compressing timelines industry-wide.
