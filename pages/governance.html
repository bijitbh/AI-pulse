FLI's President and CEO on Trump's support for an AI 'kill switch'

Future of Life Inst. Thu, 16 Apr 2026

President Trump, in an interview aired on Fox Business, stated that there 'should be' a kill switch for AI when directly asked about the need for one. This represents a notable shift in presidential rhetoric toward AI safety, aligning — at least rhetorically — with longstanding concerns raised by AI safety researchers and advocacy organizations. The Future of Life Institute's President and CEO issued a formal response to Trump's comments, addressing the significance of this development for the broader AI governance landscape. The statement comes amid growing bipartisan attention to the risks posed by increasingly powerful AI systems. A presidential endorsement of emergency shutdown capabilities could lend momentum to legislative efforts requiring such mechanisms by law. FLI has long advocated for robust safety measures, including the ability to halt dangerous AI systems. The statement likely calls for translating the president's words into concrete policy action rather than leaving it as an informal position. This development also adds weight to ongoing congressional debates about federal AI legislation. The timing follows the White House's publication of AI legislative recommendations just weeks prior, suggesting a coherent — if still developing — executive approach to AI risk. For AI safety professionals and policymakers, a presidential signal of support for kill-switch provisions is a significant opening for legislative progress. The episode underscores how mainstream AI safety concerns have become at the highest levels of U.S. government.

Impact Takeaways

  • Presidential support for an AI kill switch could accelerate legislative efforts to mandate emergency shutdown provisions for advanced AI systems.
  • AI safety practitioners should monitor whether this rhetoric translates into specific White House policy directives or congressional proposals.
  • This signals a growing bipartisan consensus that extreme AI risk mitigation measures are politically viable, not just niche concerns.

FLI's Head of US Policy issued a detailed response evaluating the recommendations' scope and adequacy from a safety perspective. The declaration represents a significant escalation in the debate over advanced AI development, moving beyond calls for safety measures to advocating for prohibitions. Geoffrey Hinton and Yoshua Bengio, two of the most respected figures in AI research, lend considerable scientific credibility to the call. The involvement of policymakers and national security figures signals that the debate is no longer confined to the tech industry or academic circles. This kind of cross-partisan alignment on AI risk is historically rare and could create unusual legislative coalitions. For AI developers and companies building toward advanced capabilities, the political and reputational landscape is clearly shifting. Regulators and lawmakers will likely face increasing pressure to respond to this declaration with concrete policy proposals. The statement adds urgency to ongoing debates in Washington and internationally about where to draw the line on AI capabilities development.

Impact Takeaways

The GUARD Act Isn't Targeting Dangerous AI—It's Blocking Everyday Internet Use

EFF Mon, 27 Apr 2026

The Electronic Frontier Foundation has published a critical analysis of the GUARD Act, a congressional bill moving quickly toward a floor vote that would impose age-gating requirements on online tools accessed by minors. The bill is publicly framed as a targeted response to high-profile harms caused by AI companion applications, particularly involving vulnerable young users. However, the EFF argues that the bill's text extends far beyond companion apps and could require age verification for any online tool that incorporates AI, potentially including general-purpose search engines. This broad scope raises significant concerns about the practical impact on everyday internet use and the privacy implications of mandatory age-verification systems. Age-verification regimes typically require users to submit government-issued ID or biometric data, creating new data security and surveillance risks. The EFF is urging the public and practitioners to take action ahead of the imminent vote. For AI companies, the bill could impose significant compliance burdens across a wide range of products and services. The legislation reflects a broader trend of lawmakers using AI-related harms as a justification for expansive internet regulation. Critics argue that this approach sacrifices precision for political expediency, potentially harming innovation and civil liberties. The GUARD Act's fate will be an important indicator of how Congress balances AI safety concerns with free expression and privacy rights.

Impact Takeaways

  • AI product teams should audit whether their tools could fall under the GUARD Act's broad definition and prepare for potential age-verification compliance requirements.
  • The bill sets a concerning precedent of using AI safety framing to justify sweeping internet regulation that goes well beyond its stated target.
  • Privacy professionals should monitor the age-verification provisions closely, as mandatory ID submission creates significant new data security and surveillance exposure for users.
#5

Cyber-Insecurity in the AI Era

MIT Technology Review Fri, 01 May 2026

This session from MIT Technology Review's EmTech AI conference addresses the growing collision between artificial intelligence and cybersecurity, arguing that AI is not just a tool for defenders but a fundamental expander of the attack surface. Security infrastructure that was already under strain is now being pushed further by AI's ability to automate attacks, generate convincing phishing content, and exploit vulnerabilities at scale. Legacy security approaches designed for a pre-AI threat landscape are increasingly inadequate, and the session explores why security architecture must be reimagined with AI as a core assumption rather than an add-on concern. The integration of AI into critical enterprise systems introduces new vectors for adversarial manipulation, including prompt injection, model poisoning, and data exfiltration through AI interfaces. Panelists explore how organizations can build security frameworks that account for both AI-enabled threats and the vulnerabilities inherent in AI systems themselves. The session highlights the governance gap between the pace of AI deployment and the maturity of security practices designed to protect those deployments. For AI governance professionals, this framing reinforces the argument that safety and security must be co-designed from the ground up. Regulatory frameworks that treat AI security as a compliance checkbox rather than a systemic design requirement are likely to prove insufficient. The discussion also touches on workforce challenges, noting that security teams lack the AI expertise needed to effectively defend AI-integrated environments. The session makes a strong case that cybersecurity is now inseparable from AI governance as a professional discipline.

Impact Takeaways

  • Organizations deploying AI must integrate security architecture reviews at the design stage, not as a post-deployment compliance step.
  • AI-specific threat vectors — including prompt injection and model poisoning — require new security competencies that most enterprise teams currently lack.
  • Regulators and governance frameworks should treat AI security as a core requirement, with implications for audit, liability, and incident response standards.
