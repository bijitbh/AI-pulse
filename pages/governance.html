#1

Prominent Scientists, Faith Leaders, Policymakers and Artists Call for a Prohibition on Superintelligence, as Poll Shows Americans Don't Want It

Future of Life Inst. Fri, 27 Mar 2026

A landmark coalition of scientists, faith leaders, policymakers, and public figures has formally called for a prohibition on the development of superintelligent AI. Signatories include AI pioneers Geoffrey Hinton and Yoshua Bengio, five Nobel Laureates, former Irish President Mary Robinson, and Obama's National Security Advisor Susan Rice. Notably, the coalition spans the political spectrum, with both Steve Bannon and Glenn Beck among the signatories alongside progressive voices. Business innovators Steve Wozniak and Richard Branson, and cultural figures such as actors Stephen Fry and Joseph Gordon-Levitt, have also lent their names to the effort. The declaration coincides with new polling data showing that a strong majority of Americans either want a prohibition on or heavy regulation of expert-level and superhuman AI. This represents one of the broadest coalitions ever assembled around an AI governance issue. The effort signals a shift from niche AI safety concerns to mainstream political and cultural discourse. It also puts direct pressure on legislators in the US and internationally to act before superintelligent capabilities are reached. The coalition's diversity makes it politically difficult to dismiss as ideological or industry-driven. This development is likely to influence upcoming legislative debates in both Congress and international governance forums.

Impact Takeaways

  • Professionals should prepare for accelerating legislative momentum around hard capability limits on AI, potentially including statutory prohibitions on certain research directions.
  • The cross-partisan nature of the coalition signals that AI governance is no longer a left-right issue, broadening the political base for regulation.
  • Organizations developing advanced AI systems may face increasing public and regulatory scrutiny tied to superintelligence risk narratives.
  • Policy teams should monitor whether this declaration influences White House AI legislative recommendations or EU AI Act amendments.
#2

Statement: Head of US Policy on the White House AI legislative recommendations

Future of Life Inst. Sun, 22 Mar 2026

The White House published its formal AI legislative recommendations to Congress, a document that had been anticipated for months as a signal of the Trump administration's regulatory posture on AI. Among the recommendations is a call for Congress to preempt state-level AI legislation, a highly contentious provision that would override the growing patchwork of state AI laws. The Future of Life Institute's head of US policy issued a detailed response, acknowledging some positive elements while raising significant concerns about gaps in safety provisions. The statement reflects ongoing tension between industry-friendly federal approaches and more protective state-level initiatives. The preemption clause is particularly significant given that states like California, Colorado, and Florida have been advancing their own AI regulation frameworks. FLI's critique centers on the absence of enforceable red lines around the most dangerous AI capabilities. The recommendations stop short of mandating independent safety evaluations or establishing hard liability frameworks for frontier AI developers. The document is expected to serve as a foundation for upcoming congressional hearings and potential AI legislation. For compliance professionals, the recommendations signal the direction of federal AI policy under the current administration. The international community is watching closely, as US federal law could either harmonize with or diverge sharply from the EU AI Act framework.

Impact Takeaways

  • The proposed federal preemption of state AI laws is a critical compliance issue — organizations operating across multiple US states should monitor congressional action closely.
  • The absence of enforceable safety mandates in White House recommendations may increase pressure from civil society and opposition lawmakers to strengthen the final legislation.
  • Companies with EU operations should assess potential divergence between US federal AI policy and EU AI Act obligations, especially on high-risk system requirements.
  • Legal and policy teams should engage with the legislative process now, as these recommendations are likely to form the backbone of the first major US federal AI law.
#3

How to actually protect against digital sexualized violence

AlgorithmWatch Thu, 30 Apr 2026

AlgorithmWatch has published recommendations aimed at shaping how a ban on non-consensual deepfakes should be implemented within the EU AI Act's AI Omnibus revision process. The piece directly engages with the legislative mechanics of the EU AI Act, addressing one of its most sensitive prohibited practices provisions. The authors argue that banning deepfakes in law is insufficient without clear enforcement mechanisms targeting AI developers, hosting platforms, and individual perpetrators simultaneously. The submission stresses that victims of digital sexualized violence are disproportionately women and marginalized groups, making this a fundamental rights issue under the EU Charter. AlgorithmWatch calls for mandatory detection and takedown obligations for platforms, drawing parallels with the Digital Services Act's existing harmful content frameworks. The piece also highlights the risk that vague drafting in the Omnibus procedure could create loopholes that undermine the prohibition's effectiveness. It recommends that the EU AI Office be given explicit supervisory authority over deepfake-related violations. The submission was timed to influence the Omnibus negotiations currently underway in Brussels. For compliance teams, the piece provides a preview of what strengthened obligations around generative AI outputs may look like under EU law. This contribution is significant because it bridges the gap between AI Act implementation and real-world harm prevention.

Impact Takeaways

  • Organizations deploying or hosting generative AI tools in the EU should anticipate stricter obligations around detection and removal of non-consensual synthetic media.
  • The EU AI Office may receive expanded enforcement powers over deepfake-related prohibited practices — compliance programs should plan accordingly.
  • Platform operators should review their DSA and AI Act compliance postures together, as deepfake obligations may require coordinated policy updates across both frameworks.
  • Legal teams should track the AI Omnibus procedure closely, as current drafting decisions will determine the enforceability of deepfake prohibitions.
#4

FLI's President and CEO on Trump's support for an AI 'kill switch'

Future of Life Inst. Thu, 16 Apr 2026

President Donald Trump stated during a Fox Business interview that there 'should be' a kill switch for AI, marking the first time he has publicly endorsed a hard intervention mechanism for AI systems. The Future of Life Institute's President and CEO Anthony Aguirre responded with a formal statement, welcoming the sentiment while urging the administration to translate it into concrete legislative action. This development is significant because it signals that controllability and shutdown mechanisms — long advocated by AI safety researchers — are now entering mainstream US executive discourse. Aguirre's statement calls for the kill switch concept to be codified in law rather than left as a policy aspiration. The comment comes amid growing bipartisan concern about advanced AI systems operating beyond human oversight. It also follows FLI's broader 'Protect What's Human' campaign and the superintelligence prohibition declaration. From a governance perspective, the statement raises practical questions about what a federally mandated shutdown mechanism would look like technically and legally. Industry reactions have been mixed, with some developers warning that such requirements could hamper US competitiveness against Chinese AI development. The episode illustrates how safety concepts that were once confined to academic debate are now shaping presidential-level discourse. For policy professionals, this is a signal that shutdown and override requirements may appear in future US AI legislation.

Impact Takeaways

  • AI developers should begin assessing technical feasibility of shutdown and override mechanisms, as these may become federal compliance requirements.
  • The alignment between Trump's statement and safety advocacy positions could accelerate bipartisan support for controllability mandates in US AI legislation.
  • Organizations should monitor whether the White House follows up with formal policy proposals operationalizing the 'kill switch' concept.
  • International regulators, including the EU AI Office, may reference US executive statements on controllability to strengthen their own oversight frameworks.

Copy, paste, govern: Microsoft ghostwrote EU policy that keeps data centers' energy use secret

AlgorithmWatch Fri, 17 Apr 2026

A joint investigation by AlgorithmWatch and Corporate Europe Observatory, published via Investigate Europe, has found that the European Commission directly incorporated text drafted by Microsoft and lobby group Digital Europe into official EU policy on data center energy reporting. The copied amendment effectively prevents NGOs, journalists, and researchers from obtaining data on the energy and water consumption of individual AI data centers. This is particularly consequential given the surging energy demands of large language model training and inference infrastructure across the EU. The investigation documents how the lobbying process bypassed normal transparency safeguards within the Commission's policy drafting procedures. Critics argue this represents a significant instance of corporate capture at the heart of EU AI infrastructure governance. The affected policy intersects with the EU AI Act's requirements for resource-efficient AI systems and the European Green Deal's sustainability targets. AlgorithmWatch argues that without granular facility-level data, meaningful oversight of AI's environmental footprint is impossible. The revelation is likely to prompt calls for reform of EU lobbying disclosure rules and the Commission's amendment process. For compliance and policy professionals, the story highlights the gap between the EU's stated commitment to transparent AI governance and the opacity being built into infrastructure regulation. The European Parliament and civil society groups are expected to push back on the data-suppression provisions during upcoming legislative review.

Impact Takeaways

  • Organizations tracking EU AI Act compliance on sustainability and resource efficiency should note that key environmental data on data centers may remain inaccessible under the current policy.
  • The investigation increases reputational and regulatory risk for Microsoft and Digital Europe in EU policy circles, and may trigger Parliamentary scrutiny of lobbying practices.
  • NGOs and researchers relying on EU transparency frameworks to monitor AI infrastructure impacts will need to pursue alternative disclosure mechanisms or legal challenges.
  • Policy teams should watch for potential amendments during upcoming EU legislative reviews that could restore public access to data center energy and water usage data.
