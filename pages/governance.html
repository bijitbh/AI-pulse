White House working group on AI – Statement from FLI's Anthony Aguirre

Future of Life Inst. Tue, 05 May 2026 21:31:53 +0000

The White House has formally established a working group dedicated to AI, marking a notable escalation in federal engagement with AI governance. FLI President and CEO Anthony Aguirre issued a statement welcoming the development, framing it as an acknowledgment that Big Tech is incapable of self-regulation. Aguirre described the status quo as 'unacceptable,' reflecting a growing consensus among safety-focused organizations that voluntary industry measures are insufficient. The working group signals the executive branch's intent to play a more active role in shaping AI policy before Congress acts legislatively. This development follows the White House's publication of AI legislative recommendations in late March 2026, which included calls on Congress to establish safety guardrails. The formation of the group comes amid a broader national debate about the pace of AI deployment and the adequacy of existing oversight mechanisms. It also reflects bipartisan pressure building around AI risks, including from civil society coalitions and state-level actors. The working group's mandate and composition will be closely watched by industry, regulators, and safety advocates alike. FLI's framing positions the group as a potential turning point in moving from voluntary commitments to enforceable standards. The development is expected to influence both federal rulemaking timelines and congressional negotiations on AI legislation.

Impact Takeaways

  • The White House working group signals a likely acceleration toward formal federal AI regulation, with compliance implications for major AI developers.
  • FLI's explicit rejection of self-regulation as adequate sets a confrontational tone that could shape congressional hearings and legislative drafts.
  • Practitioners should monitor working group outputs closely, as they may preview executive orders or agency guidance affecting AI deployment standards.

Prominent Scientists, Faith Leaders, Policymakers and Artists Call for a Prohibition on Superintelligence, as Poll Shows Americans Don't Want It

Future of Life Inst. Fri, 27 Mar 2026 18:37:50 +0000

A landmark coalition spanning scientific, political, religious, and cultural communities has issued a formal call for an international prohibition on the development of superintelligent AI systems. Signatories include AI pioneers Geoffrey Hinton and Yoshua Bengio, five Nobel Laureates, former Irish President Mary Robinson, Obama's National Security Advisor Susan Rice, and prominent media and entertainment figures including Steve Bannon, Glenn Beck, and Stephen Fry. The breadth of ideological representation — from conservative commentators to progressive policymakers — underscores the unusual cross-partisan nature of the concern. A simultaneous public poll showed that a majority of Americans do not want superintelligence to be developed, lending democratic legitimacy to the prohibition call. The statement represents a significant escalation from previous calls for pauses or safety evaluations, moving toward a demand for a categorical legal ban. FLI organized the effort as part of its broader 'Protect What's Human' campaign, which launched in February 2026 with multimillion-dollar funding. The involvement of policymakers and former national security officials adds geopolitical weight to the initiative. Critics may argue that a prohibition is unenforceable without international coordination, but proponents point to nuclear non-proliferation frameworks as a model. The initiative is likely to intensify congressional debate and may influence the White House working group on AI. For compliance professionals, it signals that the regulatory horizon may include hard capability limits, not just process requirements.

Impact Takeaways

  • The cross-ideological coalition backing a superintelligence ban signals that hard capability limits — not just safety processes — may enter legislative proposals.
  • Polling data showing majority American opposition to superintelligence could embolden legislators to pursue more restrictive AI development thresholds.
  • AI developers working on frontier models should anticipate increasing political pressure that could translate into funding restrictions or international treaty obligations.

Statement: Head of US Policy on the White House AI legislative recommendations

Future of Life Inst. Sun, 22 Mar 2026 22:12:48 +0000

The White House published its long-awaited AI legislative recommendations, representing the executive branch's most concrete guidance to Congress on how to regulate artificial intelligence. FLI's head of US policy issued a statement analyzing the recommendations, noting that they include an explicit call for Congress to codify AI safety requirements. The publication follows months of policy development and signals that the administration is prepared to support legislative action rather than rely solely on executive orders. Key elements of the recommendations are reported to address both near-term risks from existing AI systems and longer-term concerns about more capable future models. The document is expected to serve as a baseline for congressional drafting, with committee chairs in both chambers already engaging on AI legislation. FLI's analysis welcomed the inclusion of safety provisions while flagging areas where the recommendations may fall short of enforceable standards. The recommendations arrive at a time of heightened public concern about AI's societal impacts, following the formation of the White House AI working group. For the AI industry, the document represents the clearest signal yet of what federal compliance requirements may look like. International observers, including EU AI Office counterparts, will scrutinize the US approach for potential divergence from or convergence with the EU AI Act framework. The recommendations are likely to be debated extensively in upcoming congressional hearings.

Impact Takeaways

  • The White House recommendations provide the most detailed preview yet of potential US federal AI legislation, giving compliance teams a planning baseline.
  • The explicit call for congressional safety guardrails moves the US closer to a binding regulatory framework, narrowing the window for purely voluntary compliance.
  • Professionals should assess how the US recommendations compare with EU AI Act requirements to identify areas of regulatory divergence that could affect cross-border AI deployments.

How to actually protect against digital sexualized violence

AlgorithmWatch Thu, 30 Apr 2026 09:55:47 +0000

AlgorithmWatch has published detailed recommendations as part of the EU AI Omnibus procedure, focusing on how to effectively implement a prohibition on AI-generated deepfakes used for sexual violence. The piece argues that existing proposed language in the AI Act is insufficient to protect victims unless AI companies, hosting platforms, and individual perpetrators are all subjected to consistent accountability mechanisms. The recommendations come at a moment when the EU AI Act is undergoing its Omnibus revision process, making this a timely intervention in active policymaking. AlgorithmWatch identifies specific enforcement gaps, including the lack of clear liability chains between model developers and downstream platforms that host non-consensual intimate images. The article draws on documented cases of harm to argue that technical prohibitions without enforcement infrastructure will be ineffective. It calls for mandatory detection and removal obligations on platforms, alongside requirements for AI developers to build in safeguards against deepfake generation for sexual purposes. The piece also addresses redress mechanisms, arguing that victims must have accessible and effective remedies, not merely symbolic legal protections. This intervention is particularly significant given that non-consensual deepfakes are a growing harm enabled by widely available generative AI tools. The recommendations are directed at EU lawmakers but have implications for national implementing legislation across member states. For compliance officers, the article highlights that AI Act conformity assessments for generative models may need to address deepfake misuse scenarios explicitly.

Impact Takeaways

  • EU AI Act compliance for generative AI providers may require explicit deepfake abuse safeguards, including technical controls and reporting mechanisms.
  • Platform operators hosting user-generated content should anticipate new liability exposure under forthcoming AI Act deepfake prohibition rules.
  • The Omnibus revision process is actively shaping AI Act implementation details — practitioners should track AlgorithmWatch-style civil society submissions as early indicators of regulatory direction.

FLI's President and CEO on Trump's support for an AI 'kill switch'

Future of Life Inst. Thu, 16 Apr 2026 14:04:48 +0000

President Donald Trump stated in a Fox Business interview that there 'should be' a kill switch for AI, marking a significant moment in which the sitting US president publicly endorsed the concept of a hard shutdown mechanism for artificial intelligence systems. FLI's President and CEO responded with a statement framing Trump's remarks as validation of the safety community's longstanding advocacy for enforceable AI control mechanisms. The statement called on lawmakers to translate the president's position into binding legislation rather than leaving it as a rhetorical statement. Trump's endorsement is notable given his administration's general posture of deregulation, suggesting that AI safety may be carving out a distinct political space across ideological lines. The kill switch concept refers to technical and legal mechanisms that would allow authorities to halt or disable AI systems that pose an imminent threat. FLI has long advocated for such mechanisms as a baseline safety requirement for advanced AI development. The statement highlights the gap between public political sentiment and the absence of statutory requirements for AI shutdown capabilities in current US law. This development adds momentum to the White House working group on AI and may influence the shape of legislative recommendations moving through Congress. Internationally, the concept aligns with EU AI Act provisions requiring human oversight and intervention capabilities for high-risk AI systems. For AI developers, the political prominence of the kill switch debate signals that technical interrupt and override requirements may become a regulatory reality in the near term.

Impact Takeaways

  • Presidential endorsement of an AI kill switch increases the likelihood that human override and shutdown requirements will appear in US federal AI legislation.
  • AI developers should proactively design technical interrupt capabilities into advanced systems to prepare for potential mandatory requirements.
  • The bipartisan convergence on AI control mechanisms — from the White House to civil society — suggests that human oversight provisions will be a non-negotiable element of any forthcoming regulatory framework.
