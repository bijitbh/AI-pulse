FLI's President and CEO on Trump's support for an AI 'kill switch'

Future of Life Inst. Thu, 16 Apr 2026 14:04:48 +0000

In a Fox Business interview, President Trump explicitly endorsed the idea that AI should have a 'kill switch,' stating there 'should be' such a mechanism in place. The Future of Life Institute's President and CEO issued a formal statement in response, analyzing the implications of this unprecedented presidential stance on AI control. This marks one of the most direct public endorsements of hard AI safety controls by a sitting U.S. president. The statement from FLI frames Trump's position within the broader debate over human oversight of advanced AI systems. It raises critical questions about what a federally mandated kill switch would look like in practice and which agency would oversee it. FLI sees the moment as a potential legislative opening for codifying AI safety measures that have long been advocated by the safety community. The statement also connects to ongoing congressional discussions about AI governance frameworks. Experts are divided on whether a kill switch is technically feasible and politically sustainable. The endorsement could accelerate bipartisan support for certain AI oversight mechanisms. For AI practitioners and policymakers, this signals that executive-level interest in hard safety controls is now part of the mainstream policy conversation. FLI urged lawmakers to translate this political will into concrete, enforceable legislation.

Impact Takeaways

  • A sitting U.S. president has publicly endorsed AI kill-switch mechanisms, potentially accelerating federal legislative action on AI safety controls.
  • Practitioners should monitor how this political signal translates into regulatory proposals affecting AI deployment and oversight requirements.
  • Organizations developing or deploying advanced AI systems may face new compliance obligations if kill-switch provisions are codified into law.

Statement: Head of US Policy on the White House AI legislative recommendations

Future of Life Inst. Sun, 22 Mar 2026 22:12:48 +0000

The White House published its long-anticipated AI legislative recommendations, outlining a framework it believes Congress should adopt for governing artificial intelligence. The document includes a notable call for legislative action, signaling the administration's desire to move beyond executive orders toward statutory AI governance. FLI's Head of US Policy issued a detailed statement assessing both the strengths and gaps in the White House's proposals. The statement highlights that while the recommendations represent progress, they may fall short of the robust safety measures needed for advanced AI systems. Key areas of concern include the absence of strong liability frameworks and insufficient provisions for frontier AI oversight. FLI emphasized that industry self-regulation, which the recommendations appear to lean on in part, has proven inadequate based on their AI safety scorecards. The legislative recommendations are expected to serve as a baseline for congressional debates in the coming months. Stakeholders from both sides of the aisle have begun responding, with some welcoming the federal engagement and others criticizing the pace and scope. The White House document is seen as a direct response to growing public and political pressure for AI guardrails. For compliance and policy teams, the recommendations provide an early signal of where federal AI law may be heading. FLI called on Congress to strengthen the proposals before any legislation is finalized.

Impact Takeaways

  • The White House's formal AI legislative recommendations set a baseline for upcoming congressional debate, giving compliance teams an early framework to assess.
  • FLI's critique highlights that self-regulatory approaches embedded in the recommendations may be insufficient, signaling potential pushback during the legislative process.
  • AI developers and deployers should begin mapping their practices against the proposed recommendations to prepare for possible statutory obligations.

Prominent Scientists, Faith Leaders, Policymakers and Artists Call for a Prohibition on Superintelligence, as Poll Shows Americans Don't Want It

Future of Life Inst. Fri, 27 Mar 2026 18:37:50 +0000

A broad and politically diverse coalition has issued a formal call for an international prohibition on the development of superintelligent AI systems. Notable signatories include AI pioneers Geoffrey Hinton and Yoshua Bengio, Steve Wozniak, Richard Branson, former Irish President Mary Robinson, Obama's National Security Advisor Susan Rice, and five Nobel Laureates, among hundreds of others. The coalition spans the political spectrum, including both conservative voices like Steve Bannon and Glenn Beck and progressive figures, underscoring the cross-ideological nature of concerns about superintelligence. Accompanying polling data indicates that a majority of Americans support either strong regulation or outright prohibition of superintuman AI capabilities. The call reflects growing alarm in the scientific and policy communities about the pace of AI development outstripping safety research and governance frameworks. Signatories argue that superintelligence poses existential and societal risks that no current governance mechanism is equipped to manage. The statement is part of a broader 'pro-human' movement that is gaining traction across civil society and political institutions. It adds significant weight to ongoing legislative discussions at both federal and international levels. For the AI industry, the coalition's breadth and prominence represents a reputational and regulatory inflection point. The prohibition call is likely to influence upcoming congressional hearings and international AI governance negotiations. FLI facilitated the effort as part of its broader multimillion-dollar AI regulation campaign.

Impact Takeaways

  • The cross-ideological coalition signals that opposition to unrestricted advanced AI development is no longer a fringe position, increasing the likelihood of legislative action.
  • AI companies pursuing frontier model development should prepare for heightened scrutiny and potential restrictions tied to superintelligence thresholds.
  • Public polling data supporting prohibition strengthens the political case for lawmakers to act, making proactive engagement with policymakers critical for industry stakeholders.

The GUARD Act Isn't Targeting Dangerous AI—It's Blocking Everyday Internet Use

EFF Mon, 27 Apr 2026 23:22:57 +0000

The Electronic Frontier Foundation has issued an urgent warning about the GUARD Act, a congressional bill rapidly advancing toward a key vote that it says goes far beyond its stated purpose of protecting minors from dangerous AI companion applications. While the legislation is framed as a response to alarming cases involving AI companions and vulnerable young users, the EFF argues the bill's text is broad enough to require age verification gates on AI-powered search engines and other widely used internet tools. The EFF contends this represents a significant overreach that threatens both privacy and free expression online. Age-gating mandates of this scale would require platforms to collect and verify identity information for millions of users, raising serious data security and surveillance concerns. The bill's rapid movement through Congress, with a key vote expected within days of EFF's alert, leaves little time for public scrutiny or amendment. EFF is calling on practitioners and the public to contact their representatives to oppose the bill in its current form. The legislation intersects with a broader trend of using AI safety concerns as justification for internet regulation that has wider civil liberties implications. Legal experts note that the bill's scope could create compliance burdens for a vast range of online services that incorporate any AI functionality. The GUARD Act debate illustrates the tension between legitimate AI safety goals and the risks of poorly scoped legislation. For AI product teams, the bill could impose new identity verification requirements across products not traditionally considered high-risk. The EFF's intervention highlights the importance of technical expertise in the legislative drafting process.

Impact Takeaways

  • AI product teams should urgently assess whether their services fall within the GUARD Act's broad definitional scope and prepare for potential age-verification compliance requirements.
  • The bill's rapid advancement underscores the need for industry and civil society to engage early in the legislative process before poorly scoped AI bills reach a final vote.
  • If enacted in its current form, the GUARD Act could set a precedent for using child safety rationales to impose sweeping regulatory obligations on general-purpose AI tools.

Cyber-Insecurity in the AI Era

MIT Technology Review Fri, 01 May 2026 15:54:01 +0000

A featured session from MIT Technology Review's EmTech AI conference tackled the growing cybersecurity crisis that AI is both exacerbating and reshaping. Experts on the panel argued that cybersecurity frameworks developed in the pre-AI era are structurally ill-suited to address the new threat landscape that AI systems introduce. AI is expanding the attack surface in multiple ways: by enabling more sophisticated and automated adversarial attacks, by introducing new vulnerabilities in AI models themselves, and by increasing the complexity of systems that defenders must protect. The session stressed that AI security cannot be a bolt-on consideration but must be architected into systems from the design stage. Legacy approaches such as perimeter-based defenses are seen as increasingly inadequate when AI agents operate across distributed, dynamic environments. Panelists discussed the dual-use nature of AI in security, noting that while AI can enhance threat detection, it also arms adversaries with powerful new tools for exploitation. The governance implications are significant: regulators are beginning to ask whether AI deployments meet baseline security standards, and liability frameworks for AI-related breaches remain underdeveloped. Organizations that have deployed AI at scale without commensurate security investment are viewed as particularly exposed. The session also touched on the challenge of securing AI supply chains, including third-party models and datasets. For security and compliance professionals, the message was clear: AI security requires dedicated expertise, new tooling, and organizational commitment at the executive level. The conference discussion reflects a growing consensus that cybersecurity is now inseparable from AI governance.

Impact Takeaways

  • Organizations deploying AI must treat cybersecurity as a foundational design requirement, not a post-deployment add-on, to mitigate rapidly evolving AI-specific threat vectors.
  • Regulators are increasingly scrutinizing the security posture of AI systems, meaning inadequate AI security practices carry growing legal and compliance risk.
  • Security teams need AI-specific expertise and tooling, as traditional perimeter-defense approaches are insufficient for protecting AI-powered infrastructure and autonomous agents.
