How AI Is Rewriting Credit Decisioning in Real Time

PYMNTS AI Mon, 13 Apr 2026 08:01:11 +0000

For decades, the credit and payments industry relied on static scorecards and rigid if-then rule sets designed for a slower, batch-processing environment. That foundational model is now under severe strain as real-time digital transactions demand instantaneous risk evaluation. AI systems are replacing legacy decisioning engines by processing far more variables simultaneously, including behavioral signals, contextual data, and transactional history. Rather than relying on periodic credit bureau snapshots, AI-driven models can assess creditworthiness dynamically at the moment of a transaction. This shift is particularly significant for issuers and lenders operating in fast-moving digital commerce environments. Machine learning models can detect nuanced patterns in payment behavior that traditional scorecards simply cannot capture. The ability to evaluate intent — not just credit history — is enabling more accurate approvals and reducing false declines that cost issuers revenue. Real-time decisioning also reduces exposure windows for fraud, as risk assessments are continuously updated rather than static. Financial institutions that lag in adopting these capabilities risk losing both approval accuracy and competitive positioning to more AI-native challengers. The article frames this transformation as part of an ongoing series examining structural change in financial services infrastructure. For wholesale and institutional banking professionals, the implications extend to corporate credit facilities, supply chain finance, and trade credit products where real-time risk assessment is increasingly demanded.

Impact Takeaways

  • Static credit scorecards are being replaced by dynamic AI models capable of real-time, multi-variable risk assessment at the point of transaction.
  • AI-driven decisioning reduces false declines and fraud exposure simultaneously, directly improving issuer economics.
  • Banks and lenders slow to modernize credit infrastructure risk ceding market share to AI-native fintech competitors.
  • The shift has downstream implications for corporate credit, trade finance, and supply chain lending products beyond consumer credit.

In Payments, the AI Race Is Also a Governance Test

PYMNTS AI Tue, 07 Apr 2026 08:00:34 +0000

Artificial intelligence has moved well beyond pilot programs and is now deeply embedded in core payments operations, including fraud detection, approval optimization, compliance monitoring, risk management, and personalized customer engagement. The speed and scale of this deployment has outpaced the governance structures most financial institutions have in place. This piece examines the growing tension between competitive pressure to deploy AI rapidly and the regulatory and operational imperative to govern it responsibly. Questions around model explainability, auditability, and accountability are no longer theoretical — regulators are beginning to demand answers. For large banks and payments processors, the stakes are especially high given the systemic importance of their infrastructure. A governance failure in an AI-driven fraud or compliance system could have cascading consequences across payment networks. The article highlights that many organizations lack clear ownership of AI systems once they are deployed into production environments. This gap between deployment velocity and governance maturity is becoming a material risk factor that boards and senior management must address. Compliance teams, risk officers, and technology leaders are increasingly finding themselves in conflict over who bears ultimate accountability for AI-driven decisions. The piece argues that the institutions that will win long-term are not necessarily those that deploy AI fastest, but those that build trustworthy, explainable, and auditable systems. For wholesale banking professionals, this governance challenge is directly relevant to AI use in trade finance, treasury operations, and institutional payments infrastructure.

Impact Takeaways

  • AI governance gaps in payments infrastructure represent a material and growing regulatory risk for major financial institutions.
  • Accountability for AI-driven decisions in fraud, compliance, and risk management remains poorly defined at many organizations.
  • Regulators are intensifying scrutiny of AI explainability and auditability, creating compliance obligations that must be built into deployment roadmaps.
  • Institutions that invest in governance frameworks now will have a structural advantage as regulatory requirements tighten across payments and banking.

Acquirers Say Risk and Readiness Are Slowing Agentic Commerce

PYMNTS AI Tue, 24 Mar 2026 08:00:35 +0000

The report 'How Acquirers Prepare for Agentic Commerce' examines industry readiness for a fundamental shift in payments where AI-powered agents autonomously execute shopping and payment transactions on behalf of users. Findings indicate that approximately 80% of acquirers believe their core payments infrastructure is technically capable of supporting agent-led transactions. However, infrastructure readiness is only one dimension of the challenge — risk frameworks, liability models, and merchant integration capabilities are lagging considerably. Merchants, in particular, are far less prepared than acquirers to accept and process payments initiated by autonomous AI agents rather than human customers. The study identifies authentication, authorization, and fraud liability as the most complex unresolved issues in agentic payment flows. Traditional payment authentication paradigms were designed around human intent verification, and adapting them for non-human agents introduces new vulnerabilities. Acquirers are reportedly investing in new risk models specifically designed to evaluate agent-initiated transaction patterns. The pace of agentic commerce adoption is expected to accelerate as AI assistants become embedded in consumer and enterprise purchasing workflows. For wholesale and institutional banking, agentic commerce has direct implications for corporate procurement payments, treasury disbursements, and automated supply chain financing. The article frames this as a pivotal transition moment where the payment industry's governance and technical frameworks must evolve in parallel with AI capabilities. Institutions that proactively develop agentic payment risk models will be better positioned to capture the significant transaction volumes this model will generate.

Impact Takeaways

  • Agentic commerce — AI-initiated autonomous payments — is approaching mainstream deployment, with acquirer infrastructure largely deemed ready but merchant and risk frameworks lagging.
  • Authentication and fraud liability models designed for human-initiated transactions must be fundamentally rethought for AI agent payment flows.
  • Corporate treasury and procurement functions face near-term disruption as agentic systems begin to automate enterprise payment workflows.
  • First-mover financial institutions that develop agentic payment risk frameworks will capture disproportionate share of this emerging transaction volume.

The IPO Window Is Open—A Crack

Global Finance Tue, 28 Apr 2026 13:45:20 +0000

Global Finance examines the current state of the IPO market, which is showing cautious signs of reopening after a prolonged period of suppressed activity driven by interest rate uncertainty and market volatility. The piece notes that while some issuers are moving forward with listings, the window remains narrow and conditions are fragile. Investor appetite is selective, with significant scrutiny being applied to valuations — particularly in the technology and AI sectors where growth assumptions are being recalibrated. The article specifically highlights that fears about artificial intelligence's long-term competitive dynamics are contributing to valuation uncertainty for many prospective issuers. Investment banks running IPO mandates are reportedly advising clients to price conservatively and manage expectations carefully in the current environment. Institutional investors are demanding stronger near-term revenue visibility before committing capital to growth-stage listings. Secondary market performance of recent IPOs has been mixed, further dampening enthusiasm among potential issuers sitting on the sidelines. The capital markets advisory and underwriting businesses of major investment banks are directly affected by the pace of IPO activity, making this a significant revenue story for wholesale banking operations. For banks with large equity capital markets franchises — including Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan, and Barclays — the trajectory of the IPO window has direct implications for fee revenues in 2026. The piece suggests that a sustained market stabilization would be required to open the window meaningfully wider. AI's role both as a driver of new issuers and as a source of valuation uncertainty makes it a dual factor in capital markets dynamics.

Impact Takeaways

  • The IPO market remains constrained, directly impacting equity capital markets fee revenues at major investment banks in 2026.
  • AI valuation uncertainty is a specific and growing factor suppressing investor appetite for technology-sector listings.
  • Investment banks are advising conservative IPO pricing strategies, compressing potential fee economics on new mandates.
  • A sustained improvement in macro conditions and AI sector clarity would be required to materially reopen the IPO pipeline.

US Regulators Lift Veil On Digital Assets

Global Finance Tue, 28 Apr 2026 13:07:05 +0000

The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission and Commodity Futures Trading Commission have jointly moved to provide clearer regulatory guidance on digital asset classifications, addressing one of the most significant sources of uncertainty holding back institutional adoption. The guidance attempts to delineate which digital assets fall under securities regulation versus commodity frameworks, a distinction with major operational and compliance implications for banks and asset managers. While the release represents a meaningful step forward, Global Finance notes that it stops short of the comprehensive regulatory framework that major financial institutions have been calling for. Nevertheless, large banks with institutional digital asset businesses — including custody, trading, and structured product offerings — will find the guidance actionable for near-term planning. The clarification is expected to accelerate product development in areas such as tokenized securities, digital asset prime brokerage, and on-chain settlement infrastructure. Compliance and legal teams at major banks are reportedly already reviewing the guidance to assess its impact on existing and planned digital asset activities. The piece notes that residual ambiguity remains around newer asset structures, including tokenized real-world assets and certain DeFi protocols. For wholesale banking operations, the guidance has direct relevance to trade finance tokenization initiatives, digital bond issuance, and institutional crypto custody services. The regulatory development is likely to intensify competition among banks to capture institutional digital asset market share now that the rules of engagement are somewhat clearer. Global Finance frames this as a pivotal moment that, while imperfect, removes enough uncertainty to allow serious institutional players to move forward with scaled digital asset strategies. The guidance also has implications for fintech firms and payment networks operating in the digital asset space.

Impact Takeaways

  • SEC and CFTC asset classification guidance removes a key barrier to institutional digital asset product development at major banks.
  • Tokenized securities, digital asset custody, and on-chain settlement infrastructure are the most immediately actionable areas for wholesale banking operations.
  • Residual regulatory ambiguity around tokenized real-world assets and DeFi remains, requiring continued legal and compliance vigilance.
  • Banks that move quickly to build compliant institutional digital asset infrastructure will have a first-mover advantage as the regulatory framework continues to solidify.
