How AI Is Rewriting Credit Decisioning in Real Time

PYMNTS AI Mon, 13 Apr 2026 08:01:11 +0000

For decades, credit and payment decisions have been governed by static scorecards and rigid 'if-then' rule systems designed for a slower, largely offline payments environment. That foundational model is now under significant strain as digital transaction volumes surge and consumer expectations for instant decisioning grow. AI-driven credit decisioning enables issuers to evaluate not just historical credit data but real-time behavioral signals, transaction context, and intent at the moment of application or purchase. This dynamic approach allows lenders to catch emerging risk patterns that static models would miss entirely, improving both approval rates and portfolio quality. Machine learning models can continuously update their parameters as new data flows in, unlike traditional scorecards which require costly, infrequent recalibration cycles. The shift also opens credit access to thin-file consumers who lack traditional credit histories but demonstrate responsible financial behaviors through alternative data. Real-time AI decisioning is proving especially impactful in buy now, pay later, embedded lending, and instant personal loan verticals where speed is a competitive differentiator. However, the transition introduces model risk management challenges, as AI systems can be harder to audit and explain to regulators than rules-based predecessors. Fair lending compliance is a critical concern, with regulators scrutinizing whether AI models introduce or amplify bias in credit outcomes. Financial institutions are investing heavily in explainability tools and model governance frameworks to satisfy both internal risk teams and external supervisors. The evolution of credit decisioning is forcing a fundamental rethinking of underwriting talent, technology infrastructure, and regulatory strategy across the lending industry.

Impact Takeaways

  • Static credit scorecards are being replaced by continuously learning AI models that evaluate risk, intent, and context at transaction speed.
  • Lenders using real-time AI decisioning can improve approval rates and reduce default risk by incorporating behavioral and alternative data signals.
  • Compliance teams must address heightened regulatory scrutiny around model explainability and fair lending when deploying AI underwriting systems.
  • Thin-file and underserved borrowers may gain expanded credit access as AI unlocks alternative data sources beyond traditional bureau scores.

In Payments, the AI Race Is Also a Governance Test

PYMNTS AI Tue, 07 Apr 2026 08:00:34 +0000

Artificial intelligence has moved well beyond pilot programs in the payments industry, now actively shaping fraud detection, transaction approvals, compliance monitoring, risk management, and real-time customer personalization. The pace of adoption has been unusually rapid, creating a widening gap between AI's operational footprint and the governance structures designed to oversee it. Payments executives are increasingly confronting fundamental questions about accountability: when an AI system makes a decision that harms a customer or triggers a regulatory violation, who is responsible? Current governance frameworks at many institutions were designed for human-led decision chains and are poorly suited to systems that learn, adapt, and act autonomously. Regulators across jurisdictions are beginning to demand clearer documentation of AI model logic, training data provenance, and ongoing performance monitoring in payments contexts. The stakes are particularly high in payments because errors or biases in AI systems can affect millions of transactions and consumers within hours. Companies that have invested in mature AI governance — including model risk management, bias testing, and audit trails — are finding they have a competitive advantage in regulatory conversations. Conversely, firms that raced to deploy AI without corresponding governance investment are now facing remediation costs and heightened supervisory scrutiny. The article argues that the governance dimension of AI in payments is not a compliance checkbox but a strategic capability that will determine long-term viability. Industry bodies and standard-setting organizations are beginning to draft AI-specific guidance for payments firms, though consensus frameworks remain nascent. The companies that treat AI governance as a board-level priority, rather than a back-office concern, are positioning themselves most effectively for a tightening regulatory environment.

Impact Takeaways

  • AI governance is emerging as a strategic differentiator in payments, with well-governed firms gaining an edge in regulatory relationships and operational resilience.
  • Existing model risk management frameworks are inadequate for autonomous AI systems and must be redesigned for adaptive, continuously learning models.
  • Payments firms face growing regulatory pressure to document AI decision logic, training data, and ongoing performance across fraud, compliance, and risk applications.
  • Board-level accountability for AI systems is becoming an expectation, not an option, as regulators scrutinize governance structures across the industry.

Acquirers Say Risk and Readiness Are Slowing Agentic Commerce

PYMNTS AI Tue, 24 Mar 2026 08:00:35 +0000

A new PYMNTS research report titled 'How Acquirers Prepare for Agentic Commerce' reveals that the majority of payment acquirers believe their core infrastructure is technically capable of handling AI agent-initiated transactions, yet widespread adoption remains constrained by risk and readiness gaps. Agentic commerce refers to AI-powered systems that autonomously browse, select, and complete purchases on behalf of consumers or businesses without direct human intervention at the point of transaction. While acquirers express confidence in their technical foundations, many acknowledge that risk management frameworks, fraud detection systems, and authentication protocols were designed for human-initiated payments and may not be adequate for agent-driven flows. Merchants, in particular, are identified as lagging significantly behind acquirers in readiness, lacking both the technical integrations and internal policies needed to reliably accept and verify agent-initiated orders. The study highlights that authentication and authorization in agentic commerce present novel challenges, as traditional cardholder verification methods presuppose a human in the loop. Liability frameworks for disputed or fraudulent agentic transactions remain unresolved across the industry, creating uncertainty that is dampening merchant willingness to invest. Acquirers are beginning to develop new risk scoring models specifically designed to evaluate the trustworthiness of AI agents rather than individual human account holders. The report notes that the industry's ability to establish shared standards for agent identity and credentialing will be a determining factor in how quickly agentic commerce scales. Regulatory clarity on who bears responsibility for agent-initiated fraud or errors is also identified as an urgent need. Payment networks and acquirers are engaging with standards bodies to begin drafting agent-specific transaction rules and dispute resolution protocols. The findings underscore that the transition to agentic commerce will require coordinated investment across the entire payments ecosystem, not just at the infrastructure layer.

Impact Takeaways

  • Acquirers must develop AI agent-specific risk scoring and authentication frameworks, as existing human-centric models are insufficient for agentic transaction flows.
  • Merchant readiness is the critical bottleneck in scaling agentic commerce, requiring targeted education and integration support from acquirers and payment networks.
  • Unresolved liability and dispute resolution frameworks for agent-initiated transactions are creating risk aversion that slows ecosystem-wide adoption.
  • Industry collaboration on agent identity and credentialing standards will be essential to establishing trust and enabling scalable agentic payments.

Anthropic has introduced a suite of consumer app connectors for its Claude AI assistant, enabling users to link the platform directly to a broad range of daily-use services and applications. The Credit Karma integration in particular positions Claude as a potential AI-powered interface for personal credit monitoring, financial product recommendations, and debt management — functions traditionally owned by dedicated fintech apps. Since launching in July, the Claude connector ecosystem has processed significant user activity, suggesting meaningful consumer adoption of AI-mediated app interactions. The move reflects a broader industry shift toward AI assistants serving as primary consumer interfaces, potentially displacing direct app engagement over time. For financial services firms, the connector model raises strategic questions about whether AI platforms like Claude will intermediate customer relationships and capture data that previously flowed directly to banks and fintechs. The integration with TurboTax signals potential for AI-assisted tax preparation to become more conversational and personalized, with implications for both consumer experience and tax advice liability. Payment and commerce integrations within the same connector ecosystem create a pathway for Claude to influence purchase decisions, potentially affecting merchant and issuer strategies. Financial institutions not represented in Claude's connector ecosystem may find themselves at a distribution disadvantage as AI-mediated consumer journeys become more common. Anthropic's approach also raises data privacy and consent questions that financial regulators are likely to examine closely as AI assistants gain access to sensitive personal finance information. The launch marks an important inflection point in the competition between AI platform companies and established financial services providers for ownership of the consumer financial relationship.

Impact Takeaways

  • Claude's integration with Credit Karma and TurboTax signals AI assistants are moving into core personal finance functions, intensifying competition for the consumer financial relationship.
  • Financial institutions not present in Claude's connector ecosystem risk losing direct consumer engagement as AI-mediated app interfaces gain adoption.
  • Data privacy, consent, and regulatory scrutiny will intensify as AI assistants gain access to sensitive financial account and credit data.
  • The AI connector model may reshape financial product distribution, with AI platforms potentially influencing credit, insurance, and investment decisions at scale.

How AI Killed Information Asymmetry in B2B Procurement

PYMNTS AI Mon, 30 Mar 2026 15:33:26 +0000

Artificial intelligence is transforming B2B procurement in ways that go far beyond automation, effectively eliminating the information asymmetries that have historically defined supplier-buyer dynamics. Procurement leaders at large organizations now have access to AI tools capable of aggregating pricing data, contract benchmarks, supplier risk profiles, and alternative vendor options at a speed and depth previously unavailable to buyer-side teams. This democratization of information is compressing sales cycles and shifting negotiating leverage decisively toward buyers, with significant downstream implications for B2B payments and trade finance. When procurement teams can rapidly benchmark payment terms against market norms, suppliers face greater pressure to offer competitive financing arrangements, affecting the economics of trade credit and early payment programs. Supply chain finance and dynamic discounting platforms are experiencing both opportunity and disruption, as AI-empowered buyers demand more transparent and flexible financing structures. The collapse of information asymmetry also accelerates vendor switching, reducing the relationship stickiness that has historically anchored long-term B2B payment flows. Financial institutions offering B2B payment solutions must reckon with a procurement environment where buyers conduct deeper due diligence faster, making undifferentiated products less defensible. AI-generated procurement insights are also improving cash flow forecasting on both the buyer and supplier side, creating demand for more sophisticated treasury and working capital management tools. The article notes that the traditional B2B sales model — built on controlling information flow — is becoming obsolete, forcing financial services providers to compete on genuine value rather than informational advantage. Fintechs specializing in B2B payments are moving quickly to embed AI-powered procurement analytics into their platforms to capture newly informed buyer attention. For financial professionals, the key implication is that AI is not merely streamlining B2B procurement; it is rewriting the competitive and commercial logic of the entire B2B financial ecosystem.

Impact Takeaways

  • AI-eliminated information asymmetry in B2B procurement is intensifying pressure on payment terms and trade credit structures, requiring financial institutions to offer more transparent and flexible products.
  • Supply chain finance and dynamic discounting platforms must adapt to AI-empowered buyers who can benchmark financing terms against real-time market data.
  • Reduced vendor stickiness driven by AI-accelerated procurement switching will disrupt established B2B payment flows and bank-corporate relationships.
  • Financial services providers must invest in AI-powered procurement and treasury analytics to remain relevant as buyers demand deeper financial intelligence embedded in procurement platforms.
